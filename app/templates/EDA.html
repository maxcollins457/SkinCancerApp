Exploratory Data Analysis

Exploratory Data Analysis (EDA) serves as a crucial preliminary step in understanding the MNIST HAM 10000 dataset, specifically focusing on age, localization, and cell type distributions. EDA is a systematic approach to analyzing and visualizing data to extract meaningful insights and patterns. In the context of creating neural networks for the classification of skin lesions, EDA plays a pivotal role in several aspects.

Firstly, by thoroughly exploring the dataset, we gain a comprehensive understanding of the distribution of age, which is a key factor in skin lesion classification. Age-related variations in skin conditions can significantly impact the appearance of lesions, and EDA helps us identify potential trends or patterns in different age groups.

Localization information is another critical aspect considered during EDA. Different regions of the body may exhibit distinct characteristics in terms of skin lesions. Analyzing the distribution of lesions across various localizations provides insights into potential correlations and variations that can aid in the development of a robust classification model.

Furthermore, investigating the distribution of cell types, such as Melanocytic nevi, Melanoma, and Benign keratosis-like lesions, allows us to understand the imbalance within the dataset. This knowledge is crucial for developing effective strategies, such as resampling techniques, to address potential bias and enhance model performance.

Age distribution

The histogram of the distribution of ages reveals a relatively symmetrical and bell-shaped pattern, suggesting a typical distribution. The peak, or mode, is centered around the age of 45, with a substantial number of individuals clustered in this age group.

The frequencies gradually decrease on either side of the mode, with the distribution exhibiting a gradual decline towards both younger and older ages. Notably, there is a notable concentration in the age range between 40 and 65, indicating a significant demographic within this dataset.

Localization distribution

The bar chart representing the distribution of skin lesion localizations provides a clear visualization of the prevalence across various anatomical regions. The 'back' and 'lower extremity' regions stand out as the most common locations, with frequencies of 2192 and 2077, respectively. These regions are followed by 'trunk' and 'upper extremity,' indicating a significant occurrence in these broader body areas.

'Abdomen,' 'face,' and 'chest' exhibit moderate frequencies, while 'foot,' 'unknown,' 'neck,' 'scalp,' 'hand,' 'ear,' 'genital,' and 'acral' regions show lower frequencies, suggesting a less frequent occurrence in these specific areas.

Lesion type by age distribution

Melanocytci nevi is the most common class by far and has a very different distribution to the other classes. It has a balanced distribution, with a peak between 40-50. Interestingly, despite being the most prevelant class overall, in ages over 80, it is not as common as the others.

For the other classes, the distribution is a left (or negative) skew, with peaks between 60-80. These calsses also have almost no cases in ages below 20. This could be useful in the multi-input model, as according to this dataset, if the patient is under 20 the lesion is most likely Melanocyctic nevi. It would be important to check if this is the case in the population as a whole, or juts a feature of this dataset.

Cell type distribution

The vast majority of the lesions are classified as Melanocytic nevi. This is then followed by Melanoma and Benign keratosis-like lesions.

This shows that the dataset is imbalanced with regard to the target variable (lesion type). This is what alerted me to the fact that resampling would likely be necessary to achieve good model performance.

Cancer distribution

Only two of the lesion classifications present in the dataset are cancerous. These are: Basal cell carcinoma and Melanoma.

Together, these represent around 16% of the dataset. Therefore, for the binary classification model, the dataset is still imbalanced.

Cell types by sex

The distribution of lesion types for females is similar to the overall distribution, with the slight exception that Benign keratosis-like lesions are more common than Melanoma.

The distribution of lesion types for males is similar to the overall distribution.

Those with unkown sex make up a small part of the datatset (0.6%). As such, there is not enough data here to draw any conclusions.
